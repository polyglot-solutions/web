What is Parole in Place (PIP)?

Parole in Place (PIP) is a form of immigration parole that allows certain undocumented family members of U.S. military personnel, veterans, and enlistees to stay in the United States legally. Similar to the DACA and TPS programs, it provides temporary legal status, work authorization, and protection from deportation.

Who qualifies for PIP?

- Undocumented family members of active duty members of the U.S. Armed Forces, veterans, and individuals in the U.S. military’s reserve forces.

- Undocumented spouses of U.S. citizens who have lived in the United States for at least 10 years as of June 17, 2024.

While these are the primary groups eligible for PIP, there may be other circumstances in which parole can be granted on a case-by-case basis. The decision to grant parole ultimately rests with the discretion of the U.S. government and USCIS.

How Long Does Parole in Place Last?

Parole in Place (PIP) typically allows one year of authorized stay in the U.S., but it can be extended or renewed depending on individual circumstances and the discretion of the U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS). This flexibility is important because the green card application process can take a significant amount of time, and PIP ensures that individuals are protected from deportation during this period. However, it’s meant to be a temporary solution and is designed to facilitate the process of obtaining a green card rather than a permanent status in itself. Because PIP is a discretionary program, the decision to grant or renew parole rests solely with the U.S. government. This means that there is no guarantee of approval or extension, and it is granted on a case-by-case basis. Those granted PIP should quickly submit their green card applications to secure their long-term legal status in the U.S.
